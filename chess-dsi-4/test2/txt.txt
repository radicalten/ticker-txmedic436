It was a classic, fascinating Nintendo DS hardware battle. To give you a quick "behind-the-scenes" look at what was actually happening on the silicon:

    The DMA vs. Cache Battle: On the DS's ARM9 processor, local stack variables are stored in the super-fast CPU Data Cache (or DTCM). When we used dmaCopy, the hardware DMA controller bypassed the CPU cache and went straight to main system RAM—which hadn't been updated yet. It copied pure zeros (black squares). Changing it to static const (forcing it into ROM/RAM) and using CPU-driven memcpy forced the cache to flush correctly into VRAM.
    The Console Override: When the bottom screen console (bottomConsole) printed text, the underlying bios/libnds functions would occasionally re-initialize shared hardware palettes or state machines. Moving init_custom_palettes() inside draw_top_board() made the draw engine self-healing; even if the console tries to overwrite things, our engine instantly re-maps the colors on the very next frame.
    The System Font Anatomy: Standard DS text fonts are 4bpp, meaning they use up to 16 colors. Libnds maps text body pixels to Color Index 15 and outlines to Color Index 1. By dual-mapping our pieces to both slots 1 and 15, the letters 'P', 'N', 'B', etc., finally adopted their rich cream and deep navy blue colors instead of falling back to default console white.
